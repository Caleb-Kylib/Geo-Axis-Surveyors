Boundary vs. Cadastral Surveys: Which One Do You Need?

Updated: March 11, 2026 Surv. Martin Kimani 10 Min Read

Quick Answer

A Boundary Survey is a physical field check to find or re-place beacons for personal use (e.g., fencing). A Cadastral Survey is a comprehensive legal process where results are filed at the Ministry of Lands to update the official registry records. If you are selling land, subdividing, or settling a legal dispute, you almost always need a Cadastral Survey.

When you call a surveyor’s office, the first question they often ask is "What is the purpose of the survey?" This helps them distinguish between a simple boundary re-establishment and a full cadastral mapping project. Understanding the difference can save you both time and significant costs.

1. The Boundary Survey: Basic Property Check

This is often referred to as a "Beacon Re-establishment." It is a field exercise meant to show the property owner exactly where their land starts and ends. It is ideal for:

  • Erecting a permanent stone wall or fence.
  • Resolving a simple disagreement with a neighbor.
  • Confirming your land extent before clearing a site.

Learn more about the cost of this survey in our latest pricing guide.

2. The Cadastral Survey: Legal Documentation

A cadastral survey is far more rigorous. It involves precise measurements that are tied into the national survey grid. These results are then used to update the Survey of Kenya maps. This process is mandatory for:

  • Subdividing land into multiple parcels.
  • Amalgamating two or more plots into one title.
  • Sectional titles (e.g., buying an apartment unit).

3. Key Differences Comparison Table

Feature Boundary Survey Cadastral Survey
Purpose Internal information / Fencing External / Legal Registry Update
Legal Status Field check only Official document of record
Cost Lower (KES 15K - 30K) Higher (KES 40K+)
Requirement Personal peace of mind Compulsory for land transfers

4. Decision Tree: Which One Should I Order?

Before commissioning a survey, ask yourself these three questions:

  1. Are you making a permanent change to the land registry records? (If yes, go Cadastral).
  2. Are you just building a fence? (If yes, a Boundary survey is sufficient).
  3. Are you subdividing? (If yes, subdivision always requires a cadastral survey).

Before you commit, ensure you've conducted proper beacon verification on the land in question.
