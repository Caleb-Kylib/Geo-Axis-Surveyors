What to Expect During a Land Survey

Updated: February 5, 2026 Surv. Martin Kimani 7 Min Read

Quick Answer

A land survey is a highly technical 3-step process: 1. Desktop Research (checking official maps); 2. Field Work (physical measurement with total stations/GNSS); and 3. Analysis/Certification. For most residential plots, the field exercise takes 2-4 hours, while full documentation can take 3-10 business days.

In This Article:
  • Phase 1: Research & Preparation
  • Phase 2: The Field Exercise
  • Phase 3: Analysis & Reporting
  • Tips for a Smooth Survey Day

Most landowners only interact with a surveyor once or twice in their lives. Understanding what happens behind the scenes ensures you aren't frustrated by the timeline or the technical jargon.

Phase 1: Research & Preparation

Before any equipment is loaded, our surveyors visit the Survey of Kenya archives or the digital ArdhiSasa portal. We extract the original RIM (Registry Index Map) or Survey Plan for your property. This allows us to know exactly where your boundaries should be according to the law.

Phase 2: The Field Exercise

This is the part you see. Our team arrives with GNSS (Satellite) receivers or Total Stations. We look for "Control Points"—known coordinates in your area—to calibrate our equipment. Then, we locate your existing beacons or re-establish missing ones.

Phase 3: Analysis & Reporting

Back at the office, we download the raw data. We cross-reference the field measurements with the historical records. If everything matches, we issue a Certificate of Verification or prepare Mutation Forms for subdivision projects.

Tips for a Smooth Survey Day

  • Notify Neighbors: It helps if neighbors know we will be working near their fences.
  • Clear the Lines: If your boundary is heavily overgrown, clearing bushes beforehand speeds up the work.
  • Have Documents Ready: Keep a copy of your title deed or allotment letter handy for the lead surveyor.
