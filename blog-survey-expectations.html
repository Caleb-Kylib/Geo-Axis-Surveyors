What to Expect During a Land Survey

Updated: February 5, 2026 Surv. Martin Kimani 7 Min Read

Quick Answer

A land survey is a highly technical 3-step process: 1. Desktop Research (checking official maps); 2. Field Work (physical measurement with total stations/GNSS); and 3. Analysis/Certification. For most residential plots, the field exercise takes 2-4 hours, while full documentation can take 3-10 business days.

In This Article:
  • Phase 1: Deep Desktop Research
  • Phase 2: On-Site Field Measurements
  • Phase 3: Data Analysis & Beacon Certification
  • Homeowner Checklist: How to Prepare

Commissioning a land survey can feel like a black box if you've never done it before. Whether you're settling a dispute or preparing for construction, knowing the drill ensures the process goes smoothly.

Phase 1: Deep Desktop Research

Before our crew sets foot on your property, we spend time at the office and the Survey of Kenya. We pull the original subdivision maps, mutation forms, and deed plans. This research defines where your boundaries should be according to the law.

Phase 2: On-Site Field Measurements

This is the part you'll see. Our field crew arrives with high-precision total stations and GNSS (Global Navigation Satellite System) receivers. We establish reference points (control points) and kemudian locate or re-place the physical beacons at the corners of your land.

Avoid costly construction mistakes. Get a certified professional to mark your site boundaries accurately.

Check Availability

Phase 3: Data Analysis & Beacon Certification

Back at the office, we download the digital coordinates from the instruments. We compare the ground measurements with the legal records. If there are discrepancies, we advise you on the next steps. Finally, we issue a Beacon Certificate which proves your boundaries have been verified.

Curious about what this costs? Review our detailed pricing guide.

Homeowner Checklist: How to Prepare

  • Clear the boundary paths: Ensure surveyors can walk and see across the property lines.
  • Notify neighbors: It's always good practice (and avoids many conflicts) to let neighbors know a survey is taking place.
  • Have your papers ready: Keep a copy of your title deed or certificate of lease handy for the surveyors.

For more legal context, read our article on Kenya Land Ownership Laws.
