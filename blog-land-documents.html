Documents Required When Buying Land in Kenya

Updated: March 15, 2026 GeoAxis Legal Team 8 Min Read

Quick Answer

To buy land in Kenya, you need four categories of documents: 1. Proof of Ownership (Title Deed, Search Certificate); 2. Seller & Buyer IDs (ID copies, KRA PINs); 3. Clearance Certificates (Land Rates, Land Rent); and 4. Transfer Documents (Transfer Forms, Consent to Transfer).

  • Identification of Parties

Missing a single document can stall a land transfer for months or even lead to legal disputes. This guide outlines the essential paperwork every buyer must verify before releasing payment.

1. Proof of Ownership Documents

The foundation of any land transaction is proof that the seller actually owns the land they are selling. You must demand the following:

  • Original Title Deed: Do not rely on photocopies. The original document is required for verification and eventual transfer.
  • Official Search Certificate: A fresh search from the Land Registry (via ArdhiSasa) confirming the current owner and any encumbrances.
  • Registry Index Map (RIM): Shows the relative position of the land parcel in relation to its neighbors.

2. Identification of Parties

Both the buyer and the seller must be accurately identified to prevent impersonation fraud.

  • Copy of National ID or Passport: For individuals. For companies, a Certificate of Incorporation and CR12 are required.
  • KRA PIN Certificate: Mandatory for tax purposes (Stamp Duty payment).
  • Passport Sized Photos: Required for the transfer forms.

3. Mandatory Clearance Certificates

These documents prove that there are no outstanding debts associated with the land.

  • Land Rates Clearance: Issued by the County Government showing all rates are paid to date.
  • Land Rent Clearance: For leasehold properties, issued by the Ministry of Lands.

4. The Final Transfer Packet

Once the due diligence is done, these documents finalize the change of ownership:

  • Transfer Forms: Duly filled and signed by both parties.
  • Consent to Transfer: From the Land Control Board (for agricultural land) or the Commissioner of Lands (for leaseholds).
  • Valuation Report: Prepared by a Government Valuer to determine the Stamp Duty payable.
